\begin{abstract}
A $\lambda$-term's Berarducci tree is the readout of a single first-order
relation: the weak-head \emph{shape} relation $\Sh$ over the positions of the
term. $\Sh$'s clauses are positive, so
its consequence operator is monotone with a Kleene ascent $T_P\!\uparrow\!1 \sqsubseteq
\cdots \sqsubseteq \mathrm{lfp}$. Two points of this ascent give two readings. The
first iterate $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ is the first approximation: a position whose value is
demanded while it is still being computed (a re-entry) is answered by $\bot$, so a
cycle is cut to $\bot$. The least fixpoint $\mathrm{lfp}$ is the Berarducci tree
itself; because positions are interned, so that structural equality is pointer
equality, it \emph{folds} a guarded cycle into a finite rational graph: a lazy
infinite list, written with $Y$, becomes a node that points to itself. So the least
fixpoint is more defined than the first approximation exactly at guarded cycles, and
a single readout terminates iff the tree is rational (finitely many positions): an
infinite but rational tree folds to a finite graph, while a non-rational one, the
stream of all naturals, does not. On the rational fragment, contextual equivalence
is decidable.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================
The $\lambda$-calculus is the higher-order calculus, yet its tree semantics
factors through a \emph{first-order} relation. We make this precise: the
Berarducci tree $\BeT(M)$ of a term $M$ is the coinductive readout of a single
relation $\Sh$, the \emph{weak-head shape} of a position, which is first-order
in that it ranges over positions (graph nodes) and never over a function space.

Once the tree is presented this way, two observations become immediate. First,
$\Sh$'s clauses are positive, so the immediate-consequence operator $T_P$ that reads
off the tree is monotone, with a Kleene ascent $\bot \sqsubseteq T_P\!\uparrow\!1
\sqsubseteq \cdots \sqsubseteq \mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$. Second, two points of this ascent are
the two readings. The first iterate $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ answers a re-entrant demand with
$\bot$, cutting a cycle to $\bot$; the least fixpoint $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is the
Berarducci tree, folding a guarded cycle into a finite rational graph. The least
fixpoint is more defined than the first approximation exactly at guarded cycles.

A deterministic calculus exposes one weak-head constructor at a position. The value
at a position is therefore a \emph{partial}
Berarducci tree, a tree with $\bot$ holes, and each iterate fills the holes of the
previous one until the tree stops growing. An unproductive cycle has nothing to fill
its hole, so it stays $\bot$ under every iterate, the first approximation and the
limit alike. The bottom is $\bot$, and Berarducci-tree equivalence is fully abstract
for the limit.

The reason any of this is effective is the word in the title. The merge must
recognise when it has returned to a position it is already computing, both to fold
a cycle and to memoise, and recognising a repeated position is a test of
\emph{identity}. On a first-order domain identity is equality of positions, a
comparison of finite paths decidable by a pointer test, and \emph{interning} the
positions makes that pointer test exact; on the higher-order values of the
$\lambda$-calculus identity is $\beta$-equality, which is undecidable. Reading the
tree off a first-order relation is precisely what makes memoisation and the folding
of cyclic structures computable; the higher-order view affords neither.
``First-order'' is not a description of $\Sh$ but the mechanism that the results
rest on.

We never normalise to an intermediate form; in particular we do not use A-normal
form. Positions are read directly off the term, and the dynamics live entirely in
$\Sh$.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A presentation of the Berarducci tree as the readout of a first-order
        shape relation $\Sh$ (Sections~\ref{sec:firstorder}, \ref{sec:grounding}).
  \item Two resolutions of $\Sh$, two points of the monotone ascent: the
        first-iteration approximation $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ and the least fixpoint
        $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$, which we show is the Berarducci tree
        (Sections~\ref{sec:tworeadings}, \ref{sec:grounding}, \ref{sec:valrec}).
  \item The ascent $T_P\!\uparrow\!1 \sqsubseteq \mathrm{lfp}(T_P) = \BeT$
        (Section~\ref{sec:bridge}): the two agree on acyclic terms and on unproductive
        cycles ($\bot$), and $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is more defined exactly at a guarded cycle, where
        $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ cuts to $\bot$ and $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ folds into the rational subtree. Whether a
        readout terminates is a separate matter, governed by rationality
        (Remark~\ref{rem:termination}): finitely many interned positions iff the tree
        is rational. Once $\Sh$ is given its positive presentation
        (Remark~\ref{rem:positivity}), well-definedness of the least fixpoint is a
        standard fixpoint fact.
  \item As an application, structural computation over cyclic data
        (Section~\ref{sec:application}). Interning the positions folds a guarded
        cyclic datum into a finite rational tree, so an \emph{ordinary} recursive
        query over it terminates where head reduction diverges: the query's own
        recursive applications intern and fold.
\end{itemize}

\section{Overview}\label{sec:overview}
% =====================================================================
Consider a cyclic stream of zeros, written $r = \mathtt{cons}\;0\;r$, and the pure
$\lambda$-term $Y\,(\mathtt{cons}\;0)$, which has the \emph{same} Berarducci tree
(the infinite stream $\mathtt{cons}\;0\;(\mathtt{cons}\;0\;\cdots)$). Head reduction
enumerates this tree the same way in both cases, and never finishes.

The least fixpoint folds it. Once positions are interned, structurally equal
positions are one position, so the stream has finitely many distinct positions; the
least fixpoint folds the tail back onto the repeating cell, a node that points to
itself, a finite rational graph. The first approximation $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ instead
answers the re-entry with $\bot$, cutting the stream to $\mathtt{cons}\;0\;\bot$. Both
terminate; head reduction, which interns nothing, enumerates the infinite tree
forever. So at this guarded cycle the least fixpoint is the more defined of the two
readings, and both are finite where head reduction is not.

At an \emph{unproductive} cycle, a position that returns to itself before exposing
any constructor, there is nothing to fold, so the first approximation and the least
fixpoint agree on $\bot$. Such a position is reached not only by a recursive binder
($\mathtt{letrec}\;x = x$) but by self-application:
$\Omega = (\lambda x.x\,x)(\lambda x.x\,x)$ returns to the same interned position with
no constructor exposed, so $\Omega = \bot$ under both. The two readings thus differ
only at guarded cycles, and only in definedness; whether a readout terminates is
governed not by the reading but by rationality.

\section{Berarducci trees as the readout of a first-order shape relation}
\label{sec:firstorder}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Terms and interned positions}\label{sec:interning}
We work with pure $\lambda$-terms in de Bruijn form (avoiding $\alpha$-renaming);
a node is labelled $\Lamsh$, $\Appsh$, or $\Varsh$. A \emph{position} is a subterm
reached while computing a shape. Positions are \emph{interned}: two structurally
equal positions are the same object, so identity is a pointer test, not a comparison
of structure. Interning is the mechanism the results rest on. It makes the
recurrence for $\Sh$ a memoisable dynamic program keyed by position, and it makes a
position a finite key on which $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ folds a cycle: structural equality reduces to
pointer equality. This interning is the hash-consing a lazy runtime already
performs, so computing the least fixpoint is what the implementation does anyway.
A term has finitely many positions exactly when its Berarducci
tree is \emph{rational} (finitely many distinct subtrees). A recursive definition
produces such repetition directly, and so does a self-application, whose contractum
interns back to an existing position. No recursion binder belongs to the calculus:
the $Y$ combinator produces the repetition that interning folds
(Section~\ref{sec:valrec}).

\subsection{The Berarducci-tree domain}\label{sec:domain}
We fix the codomain of the readout, and the order along which the fixpoints below
ascend, before introducing the relation that drives them. The codomain is the usual
domain of finite-and-infinite trees with a bottom element; the order is information
refinement, replacing a $\bot$-hole by a more defined subtree.

\begin{definition}[Tree signature]\label{def:signature}
$\Sigma$ is the ranked alphabet with one binary symbol $\Appsh$, one unary symbol
$\Lamsh$, and the nullary symbols $\Varsh_i$ ($i \in \mathbb{N}$, de Bruijn indices)
and $\bot$. The descent alphabet $\Delta = \{\dlam,\dhead,\darg\}$
(Definition~\ref{def:path}) names argument positions: $\dlam$ under $\Lamsh$, and
$\dhead,\darg$ under $\Appsh$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Berarducci trees]\label{def:domain}
A \emph{Berarducci tree} is a map $t \colon \operatorname{dom}(t) \to \Sigma$ whose
domain $\operatorname{dom}(t) \subseteq \Delta^{*}$ is prefix-closed and
arity-respecting: for each $p \in \operatorname{dom}(t)$ the children of $p$ in
$\operatorname{dom}(t)$ are exactly $\{p\,\dlam\}$ if $t(p)=\Lamsh$, $\{p\,\dhead,
p\,\darg\}$ if $t(p)=\Appsh$, and $\varnothing$ if $t(p) \in \{\Varsh_i,\bot\}$. Let
$D$ be the set of all such finite or infinite trees.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Approximation order]\label{def:order}
For $t,u \in D$, $t \sqsubseteq u$ iff $\operatorname{dom}(t) \subseteq
\operatorname{dom}(u)$ and for every $p \in \operatorname{dom}(t)$ either
$t(p)=u(p)$, or $t(p)=\bot$. Thus $u$ refines $t$ by replacing $\bot$-leaves with
subtrees.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[The codomain is a domain]\label{prop:cpo}
$(D,\sqsubseteq)$ is a pointed $\omega$-complete partial order with least element
$\bot_D = \{\varepsilon \mapsto \bot\}$, and least upper bounds of
$\sqsubseteq$-directed families exist and are computed nodewise. Proof in
Appendix~\ref{app:domain}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{definition}[Rational tree]\label{def:rational}
$t \in D$ is \emph{rational} if its set of subtrees $\{\,t\!\restriction_{p} : p \in
\operatorname{dom}(t)\,\}$, where $t\!\restriction_{p}(q)=t(pq)$, is finite.
Equivalently, $t$ is the unfolding of a finite $\Sigma$-labelled graph.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Position set]\label{def:positions}
For a term $M \in \Lambda$, the \emph{position set} $\operatorname{Pos}(M) \subseteq
\Lambda$ is the least set of de Bruijn terms with $M \in \operatorname{Pos}(M)$ and,
for each $n \in \operatorname{Pos}(M)$: if $n=\Lamsh(\lambda x.N)$ then $N \in
\operatorname{Pos}(M)$; if $n=\Appsh(f,a)$ then $f,a \in \operatorname{Pos}(M)$; and
if $n=\Appsh(f,a)$ with $\Sh(f)=\Lamsh(\lambda x.\,\mathit{body})$ then the
contractum $\mathit{body}[x\mathbin{:=}a] \in \operatorname{Pos}(M)$. Positions are
taken up to syntactic identity (\emph{interning}), and $\operatorname{Pos}(M)$ is
generated simultaneously with $\Sh$ (Definition~\ref{def:shapeop}) as one least
fixpoint.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Rational term]\label{def:rationalterm}
$M$ is \emph{rational} if $\operatorname{Pos}(M)$ is finite.
\end{definition}

\subsection{The shape relation}
\begin{definition}[Weak-head shape]\label{def:sh}
The relation $\Sh$ assigns to a node its outermost constructor after weak head
reduction:
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \Sh(\Appsh(f,a)) &= \Sh(\,\mathit{body}[x \mathbin{:=} a]\,)
      && \text{if } \Sh(f) = \Lamsh(\lambda x.\,\mathit{body}) && \text{(head redex)}\\
  \Sh(\Appsh(f,a)) &= \Appsh(f,a)
      && \text{if } \Sh(f) \text{ is rigid} && \text{(root-stable)}\\
  \Sh(\Lamsh(\lambda x.\,N)) &= \Lamsh(\lambda x.\,N) && &&\\
  \Sh(\Varsh(x)) &= \Varsh(x). && &&
\end{aligned}
\]
A node is \emph{rigid} when its head is a variable or is itself root-stable
(non-abstraction). $\Sh$ performs \emph{weak} head reduction only: it stops at the
outermost constructor and does not reduce under $\lambda$ or inside arguments.
\end{definition}

\noindent
$\Sh$ is first-order: it is a relation over nodes, with no quantification over a
function space.

\begin{remark}[Positivity]\label{rem:positivity}
The clauses of $\Sh$ are \emph{definite}: every premise is an atom $\Sh(\cdot)=c$,
and there is no negation. In particular ``rigid'' must be read as the positive
disjunction ``head is a $\Varsh$, or head is itself a root-stable $\Appsh$'', never
as the negative ``head is not a $\Lamsh$''; a negation-as-failure reading would
break positivity and forfeit the least-fixpoint semantics below. Positivity makes
the immediate-consequence operator $T_P$ monotone (indeed continuous), so by
Knaster--Tarski its least fixpoint exists and is unique, and the least-fixpoint
reading $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ of Section~\ref{sec:tworeadings} is well-defined and sound by
construction \citeTODO{vanemden-kowalski}. Soundness of $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ therefore costs
nothing beyond fixing this positive presentation; the work of the paper is
elsewhere (Sections~\ref{sec:grounding}, \ref{sec:application}).
\end{remark}

\noindent
Definition~\ref{def:sh} is made precise as the least fixpoint of an operator on the
flat domain of shapes, so that ``$\Sh$ undefined'' is exactly the value $\bot$ at a
non-terminating weak head reduction.

\begin{definition}[Shape operator]\label{def:shapeop}
Let $C = \{\Varsh_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}} \cup \{\Lamsh(N)\}_{N} \cup
\{\Appsh(f,a)\}_{f,a} \cup \{\bot\}$ be the flat domain of \emph{shapes}, ordered by
$\bot \sqsubseteq c$ for all $c$ and no other strict relation. The \emph{shape
operator} $T^{\Sh}\colon (\operatorname{Pos}(M)\to C) \to
(\operatorname{Pos}(M)\to C)$ is
\[
T^{\Sh}(s)(n) =
\begin{cases}
  \Varsh_i & n=\Varsh_i\\
  \Lamsh(N) & n=\Lamsh(\lambda x.N)\\
  s\big(\mathit{body}[x\mathbin{:=}a]\big) & n=\Appsh(f,a),\ s(f)=\Lamsh(\lambda x.\,\mathit{body})\\
  \Appsh(f,a) & n=\Appsh(f,a),\ s(f)\in\{\Varsh_i,\Appsh(\cdot,\cdot)\}\\
  \bot & n=\Appsh(f,a),\ s(f)=\bot.
\end{cases}
\]
Define $\Sh := \operatorname{lfp}(T^{\Sh})$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Shape is well-defined]\label{prop:shwd}
$T^{\Sh}$ is monotone and $\omega$-continuous under the pointwise order, so by Kleene
$\Sh = \bigsqcup_{k\in\mathbb{N}} (T^{\Sh})^{k}(\bot)$ exists and is its least
fixpoint, and $\Sh(n)=\bot$ exactly when weak head reduction from $n$ does not
terminate. Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:shape}.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Positions are Berarducci-tree addresses}
\begin{definition}[Path]\label{def:path}
The descent alphabet is $\Delta = \{\dlam,\dhead,\darg\}$. A \emph{path} is a word
in $\Delta^{*}$. The valid paths of $M$ and the subterm $\subt{M}{p}$ at a valid
path $p$ are defined coinductively together with $\Sh$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\varepsilon$ is valid and $\subt{M}{\varepsilon} = M$;
  \item if $\Sh(\subt{M}{p}) = \Lamsh(\lambda x.\,N)$ then $p\,\dlam$ is valid and
        $\subt{M}{p\,\dlam} = N$;
  \item if $\Sh(\subt{M}{p}) = \Appsh(R,Q)$ then $p\,\dhead$ and $p\,\darg$ are
        valid, $\subt{M}{p\,\dhead}=R$ and $\subt{M}{p\,\darg}=Q$;
  \item if $\Sh(\subt{M}{p}) = \Varsh(x)$ then $p$ is a leaf;
  \item if $\Sh(\subt{M}{p})$ is undefined then $p$ is a leaf.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\noindent
A path is purely a static address into the tree being built; all dynamics (which
positions exist, where divergence occurs) are delegated to $\Sh$.

\begin{definition}[Tree readout]\label{def:btree}
\[
\Btree(\subt{M}{p}) =
\begin{cases}
  x & \Sh(\subt{M}{p}) = \Varsh(x)\\
  \lambda.\;\Btree(\subt{M}{p\,\dlam}) & \Sh(\subt{M}{p}) = \Lamsh(\cdots)\\
  \Btree(\subt{M}{p\,\dhead}) \cdot \Btree(\subt{M}{p\,\darg}) & \Sh(\subt{M}{p}) = \Appsh(\cdots)\\
  \bot & \Sh(\subt{M}{p}) \text{ undefined.}
\end{cases}
\]
The application case is retained even when the head subtree is $\bot$
(a $\bot$-headed application node is kept, not collapsed).
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Definitions~\ref{def:sh}--\ref{def:btree} are the Berarducci tree
\citeTODO{berarducci}, re-presented as the readout of $\Sh$. Two design points are
load-bearing: $\Sh$ observes a \emph{weak} head normal form (it stops at the
outermost $\lambda$), not a head normal form; and the readout \emph{retains}
$\bot$-headed applications. The first places the construction in the
Berarducci/L\'evy--Longo family rather than the B\"ohm family
\citeTODO{barendregt, levy-longo}; the second pins it to Berarducci specifically.
\end{remark}

\section{Two resolutions of the shape relation}\label{sec:tworeadings}
% =====================================================================
$\Sh$ in Definition~\ref{def:sh} assigns each position its weak-head shape: a
deterministic calculus exposes one weak-head constructor. Its clauses are positive, so the
consequence operator $T_P$ is monotone, with a Kleene ascent
$\bot = T_P\!\uparrow\!0 \sqsubseteq T_P\!\uparrow\!1 \sqsubseteq \cdots \sqsubseteq
\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$. The two readings are two points of this ascent.

The two readings are made precise as the least fixpoint of one readout functional and
as the first nontrivial point of its ascent.

\begin{definition}[Readout functional]\label{def:readoutfun}
The \emph{readout functional} $\Psi\colon (\operatorname{Pos}(M)\to D) \to
(\operatorname{Pos}(M)\to D)$ reads one constructor off $\Sh$ and defers to its
argument for the children:
\[
\Psi(g)(n) =
\begin{cases}
  \bot & \Sh(n)=\bot\\
  \Varsh_i & \Sh(n)=\Varsh_i\\
  \Lamsh\big(g(N)\big) & \Sh(n)=\Lamsh(\lambda x.N)\\
  \Appsh\big(g(f),g(a)\big) & \Sh(n)=\Appsh(f,a).
\end{cases}
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:cpo} the pointwise order on $\operatorname{Pos}(M)\to D$ is a
pointed $\omega$-CPO and $\Psi$ is monotone and $\omega$-continuous.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Least-fixpoint reading]\label{def:lfpreading}
The \emph{least-fixpoint reading} is
$\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M) := \operatorname{lfp}(\Psi)(M) =
\bigsqcup_{k\in\mathbb{N}} \Psi^{k}(\lambda n.\,\bot_D)(M)$. It merges partial
Berarducci trees from $\bot$ upward: each iterate fills the $\bot$-holes of the
previous one, and because positions are interned a guarded re-entry folds into the
finite rational representation of the subtree it generates.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[The ascent and the first approximation]\label{def:ascent}
For $k\in\mathbb{N}$ let $T_P\!\uparrow\!k(M) := \tau_k(M,\mathbf{0})$, where the
budgeted traversal $\tau_k\colon \operatorname{Pos}(M)\times
\mathbb{N}^{\operatorname{Pos}(M)} \to D$ allows each position at most $k$
occurrences on a root-to-node path: writing $S(n)$ for the number of times $n$
already occurs on the path and $S{+}n$ for its increment,
\[
\tau_k(n,S) =
\begin{cases}
  \bot & S(n)\ge k \ \text{or}\ \Sh(n)=\bot\\
  \Varsh_i & \Sh(n)=\Varsh_i\\
  \Lamsh\big(\tau_k(N,S{+}n)\big) & \Sh(n)=\Lamsh(\lambda x.N)\\
  \Appsh\big(\tau_k(f,S{+}n),\tau_k(a,S{+}n)\big) & \Sh(n)=\Appsh(f,a).
\end{cases}
\]
Then $T_P\!\uparrow\!0(M)=\bot_D$, and the \emph{first approximation}
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ enters each position at most once, so every re-entry, guarded or
unproductive, is cut to $\bot$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[The ascent converges]\label{prop:ascentconv}
$\bot_D = T_P\!\uparrow\!0 \sqsubseteq T_P\!\uparrow\!1 \sqsubseteq
T_P\!\uparrow\!2 \sqsubseteq \cdots$ is an $\omega$-chain in $D$ with
$\bigsqcup_{k\in\mathbb{N}} T_P\!\uparrow\!k(M) = \mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M)$. Proof in
Appendix~\ref{app:ascent}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Locus of the gap]\label{prop:locus}
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M) \sqsubseteq \mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M)$, with equality at every position
that is not guarded cyclic (Definition~\ref{def:guarded}). The two readings agree on
a constructor wherever the head is derivable in finitely many steps, and on $\bot$ at
every unproductive cycle; they differ only at a guarded cyclic position, where
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ is $\bot$ and $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is the folded rational subtree.
Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:ascent}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Codomain]\label{rem:codomains}
Both readings take values in the same Berarducci trees, whose meaningless leaf is
$\bot$: an unproductive cycle is $\bot$, the ordinary meaningless leaf of the
Berarducci tree.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[When a readout terminates]\label{rem:termination}
A single readout terminates iff the term has finitely many distinct interned
positions, that is, iff its Berarducci tree is \emph{rational}. An infinite but
rational tree, the lazy cyclic list $\mathtt{cons}\;0\;(\mathtt{cons}\;0\;\cdots)$
written $Y\,(\mathtt{cons}\;0)$, folds under $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ to a node that points to itself,
a finite cyclic graph; it is cut to $\bot$ after one cell under $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$. Either way
both terminate. A \emph{non-rational} infinite tree, the stream of all naturals
$\mathtt{cons}\;0\;(\mathtt{cons}\;1\;\cdots)$, has unboundedly many distinct
positions and hence no finite representation: the readout produces ever-new positions
and does not terminate, under either reading. Termination is governed by rationality,
not by the choice of reading.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Termination]\label{thm:termination}
For every term $M$, the term is rational ($\operatorname{Pos}(M)$ finite,
Definition~\ref{def:rationalterm}) if and only if its readout halts, under
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ and under $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ alike; and in that case $\BeT(M)$ is a
rational tree (Definition~\ref{def:rational}). The converse of the last implication
fails in general: positions are interned by syntactic identity, which is finer than
Berarducci-tree equality, so syntactically distinct positions may share a subtree and
tree rationality does not bound $\operatorname{Pos}(M)$ (Appendix~\ref{app:termination}
gives a witness, a rational stream with infinitely many distinct tail positions). When
$M$ is not rational the
readout enumerates infinitely many distinct positions and diverges under both
readings. Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:termination}.
\end{theorem}

\section{Grounding: \texorpdfstring{$\mathrm{lfp}(T_P) = \BeT$}{lfp(T\_P) = BeT}}
\label{sec:grounding}
% =====================================================================
\begin{theorem}[Presentation adequacy]\label{thm:adequacy}
For every term $M$, $\;\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M) = \BeT(M)$ in $D$, where $\BeT(M)$ is the
Berarducci tree of $M$ \citeTODO{berarducci} and $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M)$ is the
least-fixpoint readout (Definition~\ref{def:lfpreading}). Consequently
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M) \sqsubseteq \BeT(M)$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:locus}), with
equality iff $M$ has no guarded cyclic position (Definition~\ref{def:guarded}).
Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:adequacy}.
\end{theorem}

\noindent
Because we work on raw terms, there is no normal-form translation to justify and
no administrative-redex bridge: $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P) = \BeT$ holds essentially by construction,
and the only content is that Definitions~\ref{def:path}--\ref{def:btree} present
weak head reduction faithfully.

% =====================================================================
\section{\texorpdfstring{$\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$}{lfp(T\_P)}: the least fixpoint by merging partial trees}
\label{sec:valrec}
% =====================================================================
$\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is the least fixpoint of $\Sh$, computed by merging partial Berarducci
trees from $\bot$ upward (Section~\ref{sec:tworeadings}). Because positions are
interned, a position re-entered during the computation of its own shape is detected
by a pointer test, and the behaviour splits on whether that re-entry is productive.

\begin{definition}[Cyclic, guarded, and unproductive positions]\label{def:guarded}
A position $n \in \operatorname{Pos}(M)$ is \emph{cyclic} if it occurs strictly below
itself on some readout path, i.e.\ $n = \subt{n}{p}$ for some non-empty valid path
$p$ (Definition~\ref{def:path}). A cyclic $n$ is \emph{guarded} if $\Sh(n) \in
\{\Lamsh(\cdot),\Appsh(\cdot,\cdot)\}$, so a constructor is exposed at $n$ before the
re-entry, and \emph{unproductive} if $\Sh(n)=\bot$, so the weak head reduction of $n$
returns to $n$ with no constructor exposed.
\end{definition}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Guarded.} $\Sh(n)$ is a constructor and the re-entry sits in a
        child position. Under $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ the merge folds at the re-entry, yielding a
        \emph{rational} (regular) infinite tree, finitely represented as a node that
        points to itself. Under $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ the re-entry is cut to
        $\bot$, the first approximation. So $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is more defined here: it holds the
        folded subtree where $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ holds $\bot$.
  \item \textbf{Unproductive.} $\Sh(n)$ returns to $n$ with no constructor exposed;
        its least fixpoint is $\bot$. Both readings give $\bot$ here: the limit does
        not improve an unproductive cycle.
\end{itemize}

\begin{example}[Guarded: the cyclic stream $Y\,(\mathtt{cons}\;0)$]\label{ex:cons}
Write the cyclic stream of zeros $r = \mathtt{cons}\;0\;r$ as $Y\,(\mathtt{cons}\;0)$,
with the Scott encoding $\mathtt{cons} = \lambda h.\lambda t.\lambda c.\lambda n.\,c\,h\,t$.
Then $\Sh(r) = \lambda c.\lambda n.\,c\,0\,r'$, derived in finitely many steps
without re-requiring the head $\Sh(r)$: the recursive occurrence enters only as
data. The structurally repeating cell interns to one position. Under $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ the
merge folds at the guarded re-entry,
\[
  \rho \;=\; \lambda c.\,\lambda n.\,(c\cdot 0)\cdot \rho,
\]
the finite representation of the rational tree
$\lambda c.\lambda n.\,(c\,0)\,(\lambda c.\lambda n.\,(c\,0)\,(\cdots))$, a node
pointing to itself. Under $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ the re-entry is cut:
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1(r) = \lambda c.\lambda n.\,(c\,0)\,\bot$, the first approximation. No recursion
binder is needed; $Y$ produces the repetition that interning folds.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Unproductive: $\Omega$ and $Y\,(\lambda x.\,x)$]\label{ex:xx}
$\Omega = (\lambda x.\,x\,x)(\lambda x.\,x\,x)$ head-reduces to itself with no
constructor exposed, returning to the same interned position; so $\Omega = \bot$, the
ordinary meaningless leaf of its Berarducci tree. Both readings give $\bot$ and both
terminate: $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ answers the re-entry with $\bot$, and $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$'s
limit is $\bot$ as well, since there is no constructor for the merge to add. The same
holds for $Y\,(\lambda x.\,x)$, the pure-$\lambda$ rendering of
$\mathtt{letrec}\;x = x$. An unproductive cycle is the one place the two readings can
never differ.
\end{example}

\section{The Ascent: \texorpdfstring{$T_P\!\uparrow\!1 \sqsubseteq \mathrm{lfp}(T_P) = \BeT$}{T\_P up 1 <= lfp(T\_P) = BeT}}\label{sec:bridge}
% =====================================================================
The two readings are the first iterate and the limit of one monotone ascent. The
least fixpoint is the Berarducci tree; the first iterate is its first approximation.

\begin{theorem}[The ascent]\label{thm:bridge}
For every term $M$,
\[
  T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M) \;\sqsubseteq\; \mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M) \;=\; \BeT(M)
  \quad\text{in } D,
\]
where the inequality is the approximation order (Definition~\ref{def:order}) and the
equality is Theorem~\ref{thm:adequacy}. The two readings coincide at every position
that is not guarded cyclic (Proposition~\ref{prop:locus}); at a guarded cyclic
position (Definition~\ref{def:guarded}) $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ is $\bot$ while
$\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is the folded rational subtree, so $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is strictly more defined
there and nowhere else. Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:bridge}.
\end{theorem}

\noindent
Whether either reading terminates is not what separates them: by
Remark~\ref{rem:termination} both terminate on a rational tree and both diverge on a
non-rational one. What separates them is definedness at guarded cycles, where the
first approximation has $\bot$ and the least fixpoint has the folded subtree. The
Berarducci tree is what the merge \emph{folds} to in the limit; the first iterate is
the one-step approximation head reduction reaches before it would re-enter.

\section{Applications}
\label{sec:application}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Full Abstraction}\label{sec:variants}
Observation through the readout, place a term in a closing context and watch whether
the readout converges and exposes a head constructor, identifies two terms exactly
when their Berarducci trees coincide. By Theorem~\ref{thm:adequacy} the denotation of
a term is its Berarducci tree, so Berarducci-tree equivalence is \emph{fully
abstract}: contextual equivalence equals tree equality. Soundness is the easy
direction; the content is completeness, that a difference in the trees surfaces at
some finite path and a context can drive the readout to it
(Definition~\ref{def:ctx}, Theorem~\ref{thm:fullabstraction}).

\begin{definition}[Observation and contextual equivalence]\label{def:ctx}
A \emph{context} $\mathcal{C}[\,\cdot\,]$ is a term with one hole. A term $M$
\emph{converges}, written $M\!\Downarrow$, if $\Sh(M) \ne \bot$ (weak head reduction
exposes a constructor). Terms $M,N$ are \emph{contextually equivalent}, written
$M \approx N$, if $\mathcal{C}[M]\!\Downarrow \iff \mathcal{C}[N]\!\Downarrow$ for
every context $\mathcal{C}[\,\cdot\,]$ such that $\mathcal{C}[M]$ and
$\mathcal{C}[N]$ are closed.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Full abstraction]\label{thm:fullabstraction}
For all terms $M,N$,
\[
  M \approx N \iff \BeT(M) = \BeT(N).
\]
Equivalently, by Theorem~\ref{thm:adequacy}, contextual equivalence coincides with
equality of the least-fixpoint denotation: $M \approx N \iff \mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M) =
\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(N)$. Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:fullabs}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Decidable on the rational fragment]\label{cor:decidable}
Restricted to rational terms (Definition~\ref{def:rationalterm}), contextual
equivalence $\approx$ is decidable. By Theorem~\ref{thm:fullabstraction} two such
terms are equivalent iff $\BeT(M)=\BeT(N)$; by Theorem~\ref{thm:termination} each
$\BeT$ is a finite rational graph over interned positions, and equality of finite
rational graphs is decided by cycle detection, a pointer comparison of positions
(Section~\ref{sec:interning}). Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:fullabs}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Hard problems dissolved]\label{cor:dissolved}
Several problems that need added machinery in other settings do not arise here.
Observing sharing or cyclicity in a pure language needs an observable-sharing
primitive or a circular-program idiom \citeTODO{gill, bird}; here sharing is the
identity of interned positions. Representing a cyclic structure needs an explicit
recursion binder or a cyclic $\lambda$-calculus \citeTODO{ariola-klop}; here $Y$
generates the repetition and interning folds it, with no binder added to the
calculus. Deciding equality over cyclic structures needs a separate unification
discipline or recursive-type system \citeTODO{colmerauer, amadio-cardelli,
brandt-henglein}; here it is cycle detection over positions within the calculus
(Corollary~\ref{cor:decidable}). Terminating a recursive query over a cyclic datum
needs a tabled evaluator \citeTODO{tamaki-sato, tabling-slg}; here the query's own
applications intern and fold (Section~\ref{sec:reach}).
\end{corollary}

\paragraph{The first approximation and the ordinary tree}
The statement above is about $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$, which folds a guarded cycle and so denotes
the rational Berarducci tree directly. The first approximation $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ instead
cuts every re-entered cycle to $\bot$, so it does not coincide with the ordinary
(unfolded) Berarducci tree on the nose: where the ordinary tree continues
infinitely, $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ places a $\bot$ leaf. The two agree, and $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ becomes
fully abstract for the ordinary Berarducci tree, exactly when $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$'s $\bot$ is
mapped to non-termination. Under that reading a $\bot$ leaf is not a value
distinct from the others but the observation that the readout at that position
does not converge, which is what the ordinary tree records as a non-normalising
subtree. With $\bot$ identified with non-termination, the operational reading
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ and the standard infinitary Berarducci semantics induce the same
contextual equivalence.

\begin{theorem}[Full abstraction of the first approximation]\label{thm:fa1}
Let $\eta\colon D \to D_{\Uparrow}$ collapse every $\bot$-leaf to the divergence
observation, identifying $\bot$ with non-termination. Then for all terms $M,N$,
\[
  M \approx N \iff \eta\big(T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M)\big) = \eta\big(T_P\!\uparrow\!1(N)\big).
\]
That is, with $\bot$ read as non-termination the first approximation
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ induces the same contextual equivalence
(Definition~\ref{def:ctx}) as the standard infinitary Berarducci semantics, even
though $T_P\!\uparrow\!1 \sqsubseteq \BeT$ is strict at guarded cycles
(Theorem~\ref{thm:bridge}). Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:fullabs}.
\end{theorem}

\paragraph{Why the bottom is $\bot$, and why that is sound}
The merge ascends from a bottom value, and that bottom must be a fresh $\bot$,
distinct from every $\lambda$-term, not a reused value. Otherwise the convergence
test would be ambiguous: a position still sitting at the bottom would be
indistinguishable from one that genuinely computed that value, and a round could read
a dependency's placeholder as a real value and reach a \emph{wrong} fixed point. With
$\bot$ a fresh leaf the test is exact: a position is resolved precisely when its value
is no longer $\bot$. The only test of identity, the one that lets the merge
fold a cycle, is a pointer comparison of interned positions, not the undecidable
$\beta$-equality of values.

\subsection{Structural computation over cyclic data}
\label{sec:reach}
A recursive query over cyclic data is an ordinary $\lambda$-function. Take the naive
walk
\[
  \mathtt{reach} \;=\; Y\,\big(\lambda\mathit{self}.\,\lambda\mathit{lst}.\;
    \mathit{lst}\;(\lambda h.\lambda t.\,\mathit{self}\;t)\;\present\big),
\]
which recurses down a Scott stream looking for $\mathtt{nil}$. Applied to the cyclic
stream $r = \mathtt{cons}\;0\;r$, each recursive call builds the application
$\mathtt{reach}\;t$; because $r$ has finitely many interned positions, these
applications intern to finitely many positions, so the least fixpoint folds the
recursion. Head reduction, which interns nothing, builds a fresh application at every
step and diverges; $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ folds at the repeated interned position and returns a
definite value (here $\bot$: no $\mathtt{nil}$ is ever reached, so the walk is itself
an unproductive cycle). The query terminates for the same reason the readout does:
finitely many interned positions.
It is also memoised for free, since interning shares the repeated subcomputations.

\paragraph{Lazy, not eager}
The fixpoint is lazy, not eager: the same discipline lets a query \emph{produce} a
new cyclic datum. Mapping $r$ to a new stream isomorphic to it, with a transformed
head, the merge registers each new position before recursing into its tail, so the
result is a finite cyclic structure rather than an infinite unfolding. This
knot-tying is exactly the lazy least fixpoint; interning supplies the table.

\subsection{Memoising arbitrary programs}
\label{sec:memo}
More generally, $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is the denotation of a \emph{memoised} run of any
$\lambda$-program. Memoisation needs a comparable key on which to recognise a
repeated call; the higher-order view offers none, since value equality of functions
is undecidable. The first-order reading replaces values by interned positions, whose
identity is a pointer test, so it is the natural setting in which memoisation is
even definable. Interning is that memoisation: it is what lets a re-entry be
recognised at all. Head reduction, which remembers nothing, unfolds a cyclic position
forever; the first approximation $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ recognises the re-entry and
answers it with $\bot$; the least fixpoint $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ recognises it and folds, taking the
limit. The three are the unfolding, its one-step cut, and its limit, separated by how
much they remember.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}
% =====================================================================
\paragraph{Trees and infinitary $\lambda$-calculus.}
The Berarducci, L\'evy--Longo, and B\"ohm trees form a hierarchy by their
meaningless sets \citeTODO{berarducci, levy-longo, barendregt}; the infinitary
$\lambda$-calculus framework \citeTODO{kennaway-klop-sleep-devries} situates them
uniformly. Our $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ is the Berarducci tree (weak-head observation, retained
$\bot$-headed applications).

\paragraph{Least Herbrand models and tabling.}
$\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is the least Herbrand model of a definite program
\citeTODO{vanemden-kowalski}; the difference from $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ is the SLD-termination /
least-model gap, and a tabled evaluator \citeTODO{tamaki-sato, tabling-slg}
computes $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ by completing unproductive cycles instead of looping. Tabling is
the canonical mechanism for terminating left-recursive and cyclic fixpoint queries
that plain resolution diverges on; our query application
(Section~\ref{sec:reach}) is an instance of it transported into the
$\lambda$-calculus.

\paragraph{Rational trees and recursive types.}
The capability of deciding structural queries over cyclic structures by
cycle detection is long established outside the $\lambda$-calculus. Rational
(regular) infinite trees and their algebra \citeTODO{courcelle} underlie cyclic
unification in logic programming \citeTODO{colmerauer}, where the occurs-check is
dropped and cyclic terms are first class. In type theory, equirecursive
(\emph{regular}) recursive types give terminating algorithms for subtyping and
equality precisely by detecting cycles in the type's regular tree
\citeTODO{amadio-cardelli, brandt-henglein}. These are the same phenomenon as our
guarded-cycle case---a rational denotation with finitely many distinct
subtrees---reached by different routes. The point of difference is one of
position rather than capability: those approaches add machinery (a unification
discipline, a separate type-level system), whereas $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is a re-reading of the
$\lambda$-calculus's own tree semantics, obtained by interning its positions and
taking a least fixpoint, with nothing added to the calculus
(Theorem~\ref{thm:bridge}).

\paragraph{Cyclic $\lambda$-terms and recursion.}
Cyclic graphs / explicit recursion \citeTODO{ariola-klop} and the call-by-need
$\lambda$-calculus \citeTODO{ariola-felleisen} provide the sharing on which the
$\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$/$T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ difference rests; the guarded-cycle case yields rational trees,
and the productivity distinction is the standard one from infinitary rewriting.

\paragraph{Sharing in functional languages.}
That a pure functional language cannot observe sharing or cyclicity is a known
obstacle: a traversal of a cyclic structure diverges because referential
transparency hides the repetition. Existing remedies \emph{add} something to the
language, such as observable sharing \citeTODO{gill} or the deliberate use of
laziness in circular programs \citeTODO{bird}. We add nothing to the calculus:
interning the positions exposes the repetition, and $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ folds it.

\paragraph{Higher-order model checking.}
Deciding properties of the (possibly infinite) trees generated by higher-order
programs \citeTODO{ong-hors, kobayashi} shares the goal of answering queries about
$\lambda$-generated infinite trees, but proceeds by automata and games over
B\"ohm-tree-like denotations rather than by a least-model reading of a shape
relation.

\paragraph{Lazy $\lambda$-calculus.}
The lazy $\lambda$-calculus \citeTODO{abramsky-ong} observes weak head normal
forms but, unlike the Berarducci reading, does not retain $\bot$-headed
applications; its tree is L\'evy--Longo, not Berarducci.

\paragraph{Defunctionalization.}
Unlike defunctionalization \citeTODO{reynolds}, which compiles a higher-order
program into a first-order interpreter, we give a first-order semantic
\emph{object} for the higher-order calculus; no interpreter is constructed.

\paragraph{Position.}
The capability we exploit---terminating structural computation over cyclic
data by cycle detection---is decades old in logic programming
\citeTODO{colmerauer, tamaki-sato} and in recursive-type theory
\citeTODO{amadio-cardelli, brandt-henglein}. What appears to be new is its
position: it is obtained by interning the positions of the $\lambda$-calculus's own
Berarducci-tree semantics and taking a least fixpoint, without leaving the calculus
or adding primitives. That least fixpoint is the same tree, computed effectively
(Section~\ref{sec:bridge}): it terminates on every rational structure and decides an
unproductive cycle as $\bot$ in finite time, where head reduction diverges.

% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================
The Berarducci tree is the readout of a first-order shape relation, the least fixpoint
of its monotone consequence operator. Head reduction unfolds that fixpoint and
diverges on a cyclic term; the least fixpoint, computed by merging partial trees over
interned positions, folds it, so a lazy infinite list becomes a node that points to
itself and the readout terminates on every rational tree. Its first iterate, the
first approximation, instead cuts a cycle to $\bot$; the least fixpoint is the more
defined, the Berarducci tree itself.

\section{The Berarducci-tree domain (Proposition~\ref{prop:cpo})}\label{app:domain}
\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:cpo}]
\emph{Least element.} For any $t \in D$, $\operatorname{dom}(\bot_D)=\{\varepsilon\}
\subseteq \operatorname{dom}(t)$ and $\bot_D(\varepsilon)=\bot$, so
$\bot_D \sqsubseteq t$ by Definition~\ref{def:order}.

\emph{Directed completeness.} Let $\{t_i\}_{i\in I}$ be $\sqsubseteq$-directed. Define
$t$ by $\operatorname{dom}(t)=\bigcup_i \operatorname{dom}(t_i)$ and, for
$p \in \operatorname{dom}(t)$, $t(p)=t_i(p)$ if $t_i(p)\ne\bot$ for some $i$, else
$t(p)=\bot$. This is well-defined: if $t_i(p)\ne\bot\ne t_j(p)$, directedness gives
$t_\ell \sqsupseteq t_i,t_j$, and by Definition~\ref{def:order} a non-$\bot$ label is
preserved upward, so $t_i(p)=t_\ell(p)=t_j(p)$. And $t \in D$: $\operatorname{dom}(t)$
is prefix-closed as a union of prefix-closed sets, and arity-respecting because if
$t(p)=\Lamsh$ then $t_i(p)=\Lamsh$ for some $i$, whose children of $p$ are exactly
$\{p\,\dlam\}$, and no other $t_j$ adds a child (labels agree where non-$\bot$, and
$\bot$ has no children); the $\Appsh$ and leaf cases are identical. Finally $t$ is the
least upper bound: each $t_i \sqsubseteq t$, and any upper bound $u$ satisfies
$t \sqsubseteq u$ since at $p$ with $t(p)\ne\bot$ the witnessing $t_i$ gives
$t(p)=t_i(p)=u(p)$. In particular every $\omega$-chain has a least upper bound.
\end{proof}

\section{The shape operator (Proposition~\ref{prop:shwd})}\label{app:shape}
\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:shwd}]
Write $T=T^{\Sh}$, with $\operatorname{Pos}(M)\to C$ ordered pointwise over the flat
order on $C$.

\emph{Monotone.} Let $s\sqsubseteq s'$ and fix $n$. For $n=\Varsh_i$ or
$n=\Lamsh(\lambda x.N)$, $T(s)(n)=T(s')(n)$. For $n=\Appsh(f,a)$ the clause is selected
by $s(f)$: if $s(f)=\bot$ then $T(s)(n)=\bot\sqsubseteq T(s')(n)$; if $s(f)\ne\bot$
then flatness forces $s'(f)=s(f)$, so the same clause fires, giving
$T(s)(n)=\Appsh(f,a)=T(s')(n)$ in the rigid subcase and
$T(s)(n)=s(\mathit{body}[x{:=}a])\sqsubseteq s'(\mathit{body}[x{:=}a])=T(s')(n)$ in the
head-redex subcase.

\emph{Continuous.} Let $s_0\sqsubseteq s_1\sqsubseteq\cdots$ with $s=\bigsqcup_k s_k$.
By monotonicity $\bigsqcup_k T(s_k)\sqsubseteq T(s)$. Conversely, fix $n$ with
$T(s)(n)\ne\bot$. Computing $T(s)(n)$ inspects at most two arguments of $s$, namely
$s(f)$ and, in the head-redex subcase, $s(\mathit{body}[x{:=}a])$; each is non-$\bot$
here, hence by flatness equals $s_k$ at that argument for all large $k$. Taking $k$
past both, $T(s_k)(n)=T(s)(n)$, so $T(s)(n)\sqsubseteq\bigsqcup_k T(s_k)(n)$. Thus $T$
is $\omega$-continuous and, by the Kleene fixed-point theorem,
$\Sh=\operatorname{lfp}(T)=\bigsqcup_k T^k(\bot)$.

\emph{Characterisation.} The iterates resolve shapes bottom-up: $T^k(\bot)(n)\ne\bot$
iff $n$ has a shape derivation under Definition~\ref{def:shapeop} of height below $k$,
where each head-redex clause first resolves the head $f$ and then its contractum. Such
a finite derivation exists iff weak head reduction from $n$ reaches a weak head normal
form ($\Varsh$, $\Lamsh$, or an $\Appsh$ with rigid head). \TODO{spell out the height
measure: an induction on $k$ matching ``$T^k(\bot)(n)\ne\bot$'' with ``the head shape
of $n$, and in the redex case the contractum's shape, are resolved within $k$
iterations'', concluding $\Sh(n)=\bot$ iff weak head reduction from $n$ diverges. The
statement and both endpoints are fixed; only the bookkeeping of the measure remains.}
\end{proof}

\section{The ascent (Propositions~\ref{prop:ascentconv}, \ref{prop:locus})}\label{app:ascent}
Throughout write $R=\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)=\operatorname{lfp}(\Psi)$, the unique tree with
$R(n)=\Psi(R)(n)$ for every position $n$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:cpo}).

\begin{lemma}[Cut soundness]\label{lem:cutsound}
For all $k,n,S$ and every path $p$: if $\tau_k(n,S)(p)\ne\bot$ then
$p\in\operatorname{dom}(R(n))$ and $\tau_k(n,S)(p)=R(n)(p)$. Hence
$T_P\!\uparrow\!k(M)\sqsubseteq R(M)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Induction on $|p|$. Both $\tau_k(n,S)$ and $R(n)=\Psi(R)(n)$ take their root label from
$\Sh(n)$: if $\Sh(n)=\bot$ both are $\bot$ at $\varepsilon$; otherwise both expose the
same constructor and recurse into the same child positions ($N$, or $f$ and $a$). The
only way $\tau_k$ departs from $R$ is by returning $\bot$ at a budget cut
($S(n')\ge k$) or at $\Sh=\bot$, where its label is $\bot$ and the claim holds
vacuously. Where it does not cut, the child positions coincide with those of $R$ and
the induction hypothesis applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Cut cofinality]\label{lem:cutcofinal}
For every $p\in\operatorname{dom}(R(M))$ there is $k$ with
$p\in\operatorname{dom}(T_P\!\uparrow\!k(M))$ and $T_P\!\uparrow\!k(M)(p)=R(M)(p)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
The descent to $p$ visits the finite sequence of positions $\subt{M}{p'}$ for the
prefixes $p'$ of $p$. Let $k$ exceed the maximum number of occurrences of any single
position in this finite sequence. Then no budget cut fires along $p$ in $\tau_k$, so
$\tau_k$ reproduces the $\Sh$-driven descent of $R$ and assigns $R(M)(p)$ at $p$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:ascentconv}]
$T_P\!\uparrow\!0(M)=\bot_D$ because $S(M)=0\ge 0$ cuts immediately. The chain is
increasing: a larger budget only delays cuts, so by the descent argument of
Lemma~\ref{lem:cutsound} the $(k{+}1)$-tree refines the $k$-tree. By
Lemmas~\ref{lem:cutsound} and~\ref{lem:cutcofinal},
$\bigsqcup_k T_P\!\uparrow\!k(M)\sqsubseteq R(M)$ and every node of $R(M)$ occurs in
some $T_P\!\uparrow\!k(M)$, whence $\bigsqcup_k T_P\!\uparrow\!k(M)=R(M)=\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:locus}]
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M)\sqsubseteq R(M)$ is Lemma~\ref{lem:cutsound} at $k=1$. With budget
$1$ a cut fires exactly when a position recurs on the current path, i.e.\ at a cyclic
position (Definition~\ref{def:guarded}). At a non-cyclic position no cut fires and the
descent argument gives agreement with $R$. At a cyclic $n$: if unproductive
($\Sh(n)=\bot$) both are $\bot$; if guarded ($\Sh(n)$ a constructor) $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$
is $\bot$ at the back-edge while $R(n)$ is the folded subtree, non-$\bot$ by
Lemma~\ref{lem:cutcofinal}. So the readings agree off guarded cyclic positions and the
strict gap is exactly there.
\end{proof}

\section{Presentation adequacy (Theorem~\ref{thm:adequacy})}\label{app:adequacy}
\begin{lemma}[Meaningless leaves]\label{lem:rootactive}
$\Sh(n)=\bot$ iff $n$ is root-active in Berarducci's sense, equivalently
$\BeT(n)=\bot$.
\end{lemma}
\noindent
By Proposition~\ref{prop:shwd}, $\Sh(n)=\bot$ iff weak head reduction from $n$ does not
terminate, i.e.\ $n$ has no weak head normal form. \TODO{Lemma~\ref{lem:rootactive} is
the one external input: identify ``no weak head normal form'' with Berarducci's
root-active (meaningless) set \citeTODO{berarducci}, the set quotiented to $\bot$ in
the Berarducci tree. The two endpoints are pinned (Definition~\ref{def:shapeop}'s
$\bot$, and Berarducci's meaningless terms); only their identification is cited rather
than reproved.}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:adequacy}]
Let $R=\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)=\operatorname{lfp}(\Psi)$. Both the readout and $\BeT$ label a
valid path $p$ by a function of $\Sh(\subt{M}{p})$: $\Varsh_i$, $\Lamsh$ (with child
$p\,\dlam$), $\Appsh$ (with children $p\,\dhead,p\,\darg$, retained even when the head
is $\bot$), or $\bot$ (Definitions~\ref{def:sh}, \ref{def:btree}). By
Proposition~\ref{prop:cpo}, $R$ is the unique tree with $R(\subt{M}{p})=
\Psi(R)(\subt{M}{p})$, so $R(M)(p)$ is determined by $\Sh(\subt{M}{p})$ exactly as
$\BeT$ prescribes. By induction on $|p|$, $p\in\operatorname{dom}(R(M))\iff
p\in\operatorname{dom}(\BeT(M))$ with equal labels: the variable, $\Lamsh$, and
$\Appsh$ cases match clause by clause, and the $\bot$-leaf case matches by
Lemma~\ref{lem:rootactive}. Hence $R(M)=\BeT(M)$. The consequence
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M)\sqsubseteq\BeT(M)$ and the equality locus are
Proposition~\ref{prop:locus} with $R=\BeT$.
\end{proof}

\section{Termination (Theorem~\ref{thm:termination})}\label{app:termination}
\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:termination}]
Either reading descends only through positions of $\operatorname{Pos}(M)$
(Definition~\ref{def:positions}): the constructor children $N,f,a$ and, via the
head-redex clause of $\Sh$, the contracta are exactly its generators.

\emph{Rational $\Rightarrow$ halts.} If $\operatorname{Pos}(M)$ is finite, the
$\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ computation tables over a finite set and halts, and $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$
cuts every path at the first repeat, so its tree has depth at most
$|\operatorname{Pos}(M)|$ and is finite.

\emph{Halts $\Rightarrow$ rational.} Contrapositive: if $\operatorname{Pos}(M)$ is
infinite then the descent reaches infinitely many distinct positions, $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$
never cuts a non-repeating position, and the $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ tabling never saturates,
so neither halts.

\emph{Rational $\Rightarrow$ tree rational.} The subtrees of $\BeT(M)=R(M)$ are
$\{R(\subt{M}{p})\}_p=\{R(n):n\in\operatorname{Pos}(M)\}$, finite, so $\BeT(M)$ is
rational (Definition~\ref{def:rational}).

\emph{Failure of the converse.} Interning identifies positions by \emph{syntactic}
(de Bruijn) identity, which is strictly finer than Berarducci-tree equality, so the map
$n\mapsto R(n)$ need not be injective: syntactically distinct positions can carry the
same tree. A witness is $M=Y\,F\,\underline 0$ with
$F=\lambda s.\lambda n.\,\mathtt{cons}\;0\;(s\,(\mathtt{succ}\;n))$. The head of every
cell is the constant $0$, so $\BeT(M)$ is the rational stream
$\mathtt{cons}\;0\;(\mathtt{cons}\;0\;\cdots)$ with finitely many distinct subtrees;
but the tail positions $Y\,F\,\underline k$ (with $\underline k=\mathtt{succ}^{k}\,
\underline 0$) are pairwise syntactically distinct and never intern together, since
$\Sh$ does not reduce the $\mathtt{succ}^{k}\,\underline 0$ buried in the argument.
Hence $\operatorname{Pos}(M)$ is infinite while $\{R(n)\}$ is finite, and the readout
diverges on a rational tree. So tree rationality does not entail finite
$\operatorname{Pos}(M)$: folding by position identity is strictly finer than folding by
subtree.
\end{proof}

\section{The ascent chain (Theorem~\ref{thm:bridge})}\label{app:bridge}
\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:bridge}]
$T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M)\sqsubseteq\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M)$ is Proposition~\ref{prop:locus} (or
Lemma~\ref{lem:cutsound} at $k=1$); $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)(M)=\BeT(M)$ is
Theorem~\ref{thm:adequacy}; and the strictness locus (guarded cyclic positions, and
nowhere else) is Proposition~\ref{prop:locus}.
\end{proof}

\section{Full abstraction (Theorems~\ref{thm:fullabstraction}, \ref{thm:fa1};
Corollary~\ref{cor:decidable})}\label{app:fullabs}
The two directions of full abstraction rest on two standard facts about tree
semantics, isolated here as lemmas.

\begin{lemma}[Compositionality]\label{lem:compositional}
For every context $\mathcal{C}[\,\cdot\,]$, $\BeT(\mathcal{C}[M])$ depends on $M$ only
through $\BeT(M)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Separation]\label{lem:separation}
If $\BeT(M)$ and $\BeT(N)$ differ at a finite path $p$, there is a context
$\mathcal{C}[\,\cdot\,]$ with exactly one of $\mathcal{C}[M]\!\Downarrow$,
$\mathcal{C}[N]\!\Downarrow$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:fullabstraction}]
\emph{Soundness.} Convergence is read at the root,
$M\!\Downarrow\iff\BeT(M)(\varepsilon)\ne\bot$ (Definition~\ref{def:ctx},
Proposition~\ref{prop:shwd}). If $\BeT(M)=\BeT(N)$ then by
Lemma~\ref{lem:compositional} $\BeT(\mathcal{C}[M])=\BeT(\mathcal{C}[N])$ for every
$\mathcal{C}$, so $\mathcal{C}[M]\!\Downarrow\iff\mathcal{C}[N]\!\Downarrow$ and
$M\approx N$. \emph{Completeness.} Contrapositive: if $\BeT(M)\ne\BeT(N)$ they differ
at some finite path $p$ (the trees are equal iff equal at every finite path), and
Lemma~\ref{lem:separation} gives a context separating them, so $M\not\approx N$. The
restatement via $\mathrm{lfp}(T_P)$ is Theorem~\ref{thm:adequacy}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:decidable}]
Let $M,N$ be rational (Definition~\ref{def:rationalterm}). By
Theorem~\ref{thm:termination} each of $\BeT(M),\BeT(N)$ is a rational tree, presented
by the finite readout graph over $\operatorname{Pos}(M)$, $\operatorname{Pos}(N)$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:adequacy}); these graphs are computable, since weak head reduction
terminates at each position (Proposition~\ref{prop:shwd}) and the position set closes
after finitely many steps, while position identity is syntactic equality of interned
terms. Equality of two rational trees given by finite graphs is decided by the standard
cycle-detecting bisimulation \citeTODO{courcelle}. By
Theorem~\ref{thm:fullabstraction}, $M\approx N\iff\BeT(M)=\BeT(N)$, so $\approx$ is
decidable on the rational fragment.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Guarded cuts are observationally divergent]\label{lem:fa1}
For every context $\mathcal{C}$, the test $\mathcal{C}[M]\!\Downarrow$ depends on
$\BeT(M)$ only through $\eta(T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M))$: replacing a guarded-cyclic subtree
by the divergence observation preserves every convergence test.
\end{lemma}
\TODO{Lemma~\ref{lem:fa1} is the least certain step of the paper. It asserts that no
context distinguishes an infinite guarded-cyclic subtree from divergence under the
convergence preorder. The intended argument: a converging context exposes only a
finite prefix of its subject, which the unfolding $T_P\!\uparrow\!k$ ($k$ large)
already supplies, while $\eta$ sends the cut $\bot$ to the same divergence a
non-productive probe observes. If instead a separating context exists, the result must
be weakened from an equivalence to a preorder. We flag this as open and isolate it
here so that nothing downstream depends on its stronger form.}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:fa1}]
By Theorem~\ref{thm:fullabstraction}, $M\approx N\iff\BeT(M)=\BeT(N)$. By
Theorem~\ref{thm:bridge}, $T_P\!\uparrow\!1$ and $\BeT$ differ only at guarded cyclic
positions, where $\eta$ collapses the cut $\bot$ to divergence. Lemma~\ref{lem:fa1}
then gives $\eta(T_P\!\uparrow\!1(M))=\eta(T_P\!\uparrow\!1(N))\iff\BeT(M)=\BeT(N)$,
hence $\iff M\approx N$.
\end{proof}
